## Section A — What SE Management Is

**Q:** Define **Software Engineering Management** (formal definition).

**A:** Software Engineering Management is **the application of management activities to ensure that software products and software engineering services are delivered efficiently, effectively, and to the benefit of stakeholders.**

---

**Q:** What is "the application of management activities" really about, in this context?

**A:** Ensuring software products and services are delivered:
- **Efficiently** (good use of resources),
- **Effectively** (achieving the intended outcome), and
- **To the benefit of stakeholders** (delivering real value).

---

**A:**

2. How to become a manager
3. Manager Skills

6. Manager meetings
7. Manager delegations

## Section B — The Three Pillars of Management

**Q:** Name the **Three Pillars of Management** (the manager's mandate).

**A:**
1. **People** — lead and grow a team of talented engineers.
2. **Product** — own the technical vision and successful delivery of the feature.
3. **Process** — create a smooth and efficient system for getting work done.

## Section C — Foundation of Manager Success

**Q:** Finish the sentence: **Great managers are more than just _______ engineers.**

**A:** **experienced.**

---

**Q:** What is the foundation of a manager's success, beyond technical knowledge?

**A:** Managers combine **deep technical knowledge** with **emotional intelligence** required to head off workplace politics and create a safe, high-performance environment.

---

**Q:** Finish the sentence: A manager's job isn't just to build the **feature** — it's to build the **_______** that builds the feature.

**A:** **team.**

---

**Q:** What is **emotional intelligence** in the context of management, and why does it matter?

**A:** It is the ability to **read the room, navigate interpersonal dynamics, and prevent or defuse politics**. It matters because a high-performance environment is a *human* environment — the technical work depends on healthy relationships.

---

**Q:** What is **The Manager's Role in a Crisis**?

**A:** Problem-solving extends beyond the code — managers must **navigate organizational challenges**, advocate to upper management, justify investments, and **defend the team's work** to the rest of the organization.

---

**Q:** (MCQ) Which is most accurate about great managers?

  A. They simply have more years coding than the team.
  B. They combine deep technical knowledge with emotional intelligence.
  C. They focus only on output metrics, not relationships.
  D. They are interchangeable with senior individual contributors.

**A:** **B.**

## Section D — Three Directions of Communication

**Q:** What are **The Three Directions of Communication** for a manager?

**A:**
1. **Upward** (to Leadership)
2. **Inward** (to Your Team)
3. **Outward** (to Customers / Partner Teams)

---

**Q:** Describe each direction:
- **Upward**
- **Inward**
- **Outward**

**A:**
- **Upward (to Leadership):** Focus on **progress, risks, and resource needs.** Be **concise and strategic.**
- **Inward (to Your Team):** Focus on **goals, feedback, and removing blockers.** Be **clear and supportive.**
- **Outward (to Customers / Partner Teams):** Focus on **what the feature does and how to use it.** Be **simple and direct.**

---

**Q:** Finish the sentence: As a manager, you must tailor your **_______** for different audiences to build positive relationships and align everyone on common objectives.

**A:** **communication.**

---

**Q:** What does "Don't Forget to Manage Up and Out" mean?

**A:** Your role as a manager is a **communication hub**. You are the primary liaison between your team and the rest of the company. You must communicate to:
- **Senior Management:** submit clear, concise proposals and progress reports; keep stakeholders informed of successes and roadblocks.
- **Partner Teams** (e.g., SRE, UX, Marketing): collaborate to ensure a smooth, integrated launch.

---

**Q:** (MCQ) Which direction of communication should be **strategic and concise**?

  A. Upward
  B. Inward
  C. Outward

**A:** **A. Upward.**

---

**Q:** (MCQ) Which direction of communication should be **clear and supportive**?

  A. Upward
  B. Inward
  C. Outward

**A:** **B. Inward (to your team).**

---

**Q:** (MCQ) Which direction of communication should be **simple and direct**, focused on what features do?

  A. Upward
  B. Inward
  C. Outward

**A:** **C. Outward.**

## Section E — One-on-Ones (The Most Important Meeting)

**Q:** What is the manager's "**most important meeting**"?

**A:** The **One-on-One.** It is the manager's **single most valuable tool** for building connection and supporting growth.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between a group meeting and a one-on-one?

**A:**
- **Group meetings** are for **feedback** (status, alignment, decisions).
- **One-on-ones** are for **connection and growth** — focused on the individual.

---

**Q:** What is the recommended **cadence** for one-on-ones?

**A:**
- **Weekly** — have a 1-on-1 with each member of your team. This is for **status updates, roadblocks, and general support**.
- **Monthly** — dedicate at least one of these meetings to **staff career goals and ideas**. This is your investment in their **future**.

---

**Q:** Finish the sentence: 1-on-1s are your **single most valuable tool** as a _______.

**A:** **manager.**

---

**Q:** (MCQ) The one-on-one is for:

  A. Group decisions
  B. Connection and growth with each individual
  C. Replacing all email
  D. Performance reviews only

**A:** **B.**

## Section F — The Meeting Playbook

**Q:** Name the **three rules** of "Taming the Beast: Your Meeting Playbook."

**A:**
1. **Justify its Existence.** Only organize a meeting if it's absolutely necessary and has a clear goal.
2. **Curate the Guest List.** Invite only the people who need to be there. Respect everyone else's time.
3. **Police the Tangents.** Steer the conversation back on topic, especially during technical debates.

---

**Q:** Finish the sentence: As the manager, it's your job to **steer the conversation back on topic** — known as **_______ the tangents.**

**A:** **policing.**

---

  C. Curate the guest list.
  D. Police tangents to keep the meeting on goal.
  E. Always run for the full scheduled time.

  A. Defending the team's work to upper management
  B. Navigating organizational politics
  C. Writing every line of production code themselves
  D. Removing blockers for the team
  E. Cancelling all meetings, regardless of purpose

**A:** **A, B, D.**

---

**Q:** (Scenario) Your project is going over budget. What is your communication priority *upward*?

**A:** Be **concise and strategic** — frame the issue as **risk + resource need**, propose options, and explain the design's benefit to the organization to justify the investment.

---

**Q:** (Scenario) A team member has been quiet and disengaged for two weeks. What management tool addresses this?

**A:** A **one-on-one** focused on connection and growth. Use the weekly cadence for short-term blockers; if the issue is deeper, dedicate the next monthly conversation to career goals and personal motivation.
